Research

I'm a graduate student in UC Berkeley's Earth and Planetary Science department, who enjoys learning about our nearest neighbors. Spacecraft explore places humans simply cannot and they provide an wealth of data that elucidate the environments of such places. Currently, my research uses data from the Mars Atmosphere and Volatile EvolutioN (MAVEN) to study electron acceleration in the Martian magnetotail.

See a list of my publications.

Education

I received a Master of Science in Physics at Fisk University as part of the Fisk-Vanderbilt Bridge Program. Using the Illustris cosmological simulation, I measured the clustering properties of binary black hole mergers to determine the potential for biased structure measurements from the Laser Interferometer Space Antenna (LISA) data stream. While completing my undergraduate degree in Astronomy and International Affairs at the University of Colorado Boulder, I characterized the spatial distribution of thermal electrons in the Martian magnetotail at the Laboratory for Atmospheric and Space Physics (LASP).

About Me

I am a Guyanese-American whose path towards planetary science research has been unconventional. In my junior year of college, I upended my career plans (international affairs/economics) to try my hand at astronomy research. Working with the MAVEN team, I learned a great deal about the research process. Afterward, I obtained a Master's degree and defended my thesis on binary black hole mergers in Summer 2020. Now, I am a gradute student in the Earth & Planetary Science department at UC Berkeley studying space plasma.
My other passions involve exploring the incredible wilderness here on Earth, my favorite planet :) To be a first-generation student who comes from a low-income background, I am grateful to pursue these passions.
